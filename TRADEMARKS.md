# Kelma Trademark Policy

The Kelma names and logos identify the Kelma project and ecosystem. The AGPL
license covers software copyright; it does not grant trademark rights.

> **Status:** The permissions in “Uses that do not require a separate license”
> below are the project's current policy. The broader ecosystem trademark
> license, application process, governance, and standard terms are still being
> designed in public.

## Uses that do not require a separate license

You may:

- refer truthfully to Kelma, KelmaSync, or another Kelma project;
- state that software or a service is “Kelma-compatible”, “for Kelma”, or “based
  on Kelma”, provided the wording does not imply endorsement;
- link to the project, discuss it, teach it, or compare it with other software;
- redistribute an unmodified official release under its original project name;
- use the word Kelma in ordinary text when it is not acting as your own brand.

Do not use Kelma names or logos in a way that is deceptive, confusing, or
suggests sponsorship or official status that has not been granted.

## Commitment to ecosystem builders

Kelma would like to grant ecosystem builders extensive, practical trademark
rights—not merely permission to mention compatibility. This includes builders
of distributions, clients, hosted sync providers, integrations, documentation,
community projects, and commercial services.

The goal is for responsible independent builders to be able to use Kelma marks
prominently under clear and predictable terms. The project intends for the
standard ecosystem license to be available without a trademark royalty when
the use:

1. clearly identifies who maintains and is responsible for the offering;
2. does not misrepresent compatibility, security, or official project status;
3. preserves user freedom and complies with the applicable open-source
   licenses; and
4. maintains reasonable quality and security appropriate to the offering.

Quality-control, attribution, and naming requirements are necessary to preserve
trademark meaning, but they should not become a pretext for preventing
independent implementations or competing services.

## A different approach from Anki

This is deliberately intended to differ from Anki's trademark approach. Kelma
does not want ecosystem builders to depend on opaque, case-by-case decisions to
learn whether they may build a recognizable product or service around the
project. Protecting users from confusion and protecting the identity of the
upstream project remain important, but those goals should coexist with broad,
published rights for the ecosystem.

Kelma therefore intends to publish the standard terms, eligibility criteria,
application steps, decision process, and reasons a request may be denied. The
project should not advertise an open ecosystem while keeping meaningful use of
its identity available only through private or unpredictable permission.

## Planned Linux-style framework

The final framework will be modeled on the way the Linux trademark is handled,
rather than copying Anki's policy. The
[Linux trademark model](https://www.linuxfoundation.org/legal/the-linux-mark)
protects a shared project identity while providing a documented route for
builders to obtain the rights needed for distributions, products, and services.
Kelma intends to follow the same broad principles:

- the mark remains a reliable indication of origin and compatibility;
- descriptive, educational, and community use is broadly permitted;
- ecosystem products and services can obtain substantial branding rights
  through published, standardized terms;
- commercial and noncommercial builders are treated by the same public rules;
- quality requirements are concrete and related to avoiding user confusion;
- the request and decision process is documented instead of opaque; and
- governance evolves publicly with input from the builders who rely on it.

Kelma's exact legal structure may differ from Linux's. The project has not yet
settled questions such as who will administer licenses, whether a formal
sublicense structure is needed, how compatibility will be evaluated, or what
appeal and transition processes should exist.

## Status of broader licenses

The final ecosystem license is not yet available, and this document does not
automatically grant the broader product, service, organization, domain-name, or
modified-distribution branding rights that the future license is intended to
cover. Builders may request written interim permission through this repository.
Interim decisions should follow the public principles above and help inform the
standard policy rather than remain unexplained private exceptions.

## Modified versions

The software license permits forks and modifications. Unless you have a
trademark license, materially modified versions should use their own primary
name and may describe themselves as “based on Kelma” or “Kelma-compatible”.

## Logos

Do not alter or use a Kelma logo as your product or organization logo without
written permission. Descriptive use of an unmodified logo in articles,
compatibility pages, and project discussions is allowed when the surrounding
context makes the source and lack of endorsement clear.

Policy proposals, questions, and non-sensitive interim license requests should
be opened as repository issues so the framework develops in public. Sensitive
commercial details may be sent through a private channel arranged with the
maintainers, but the governing criteria and eventual standard terms will remain
public.
